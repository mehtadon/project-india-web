You don't need permission from anyone to start participating in your own democracy. Here's how to begin.

## Step 1: Find Your People

Look around your ward. There are people everywhere who have time and care about where they live — homemakers, retired professionals, students, senior citizens. Start with your neighbours. Have a conversation. Ask: what would you change about our ward if you could?

You don't need hundreds of people. Even 10-15 committed citizens in a ward can make a real difference.

## Step 2: Hold Your First Meeting

Pick a time, pick a place — a park, a community hall, someone's terrace. Sit down together and discuss one question: how should our ward look one year from now?

Talk about the roads, the drainage, the trees, the streetlights, the garbage collection. Talk about what's broken, what's missing, and what could be better. Write it all down.

## Step 3: Create a One-Year Plan

Take everything discussed and turn it into a concrete plan. Not a wishlist — a plan. Prioritise. What needs to be fixed first? What can be done in phases? What will it take?

This plan becomes your ward association's proposal — a document that says: this is what the citizens of this ward need.

## Step 4: Propose It to Your Ward Councillor

Take the plan to your ward incharge — the councillor, the MLA, whoever represents you. Present it formally. Ask them to act on it. Push them to escalate what they can't handle to the MP or higher authorities.

The key is that you're not asking for favours. You're presenting a citizen-created plan and demanding accountability for its execution.

## Step 5: Oversee and Document

Once work begins, the association's job isn't over — it's just starting. When a road gets dug up, ask why. Check if the drainage and water lines are being addressed at the same time. Ask who's building the road, what load it's designed to handle, and what the timeline is.

Document everything. Take photos, record videos, put it on YouTube. Transparency is the foundation of accountability.

## Remember

You're not starting a political party. You're not fighting the system. You're using democracy the way it was designed — citizens telling their representatives what they need, and holding them to it. One ward at a time.
