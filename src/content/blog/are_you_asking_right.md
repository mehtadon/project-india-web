In September 2024, nearly 2 lakh government employees in Himachal Pradesh did not receive their salaries. No flood. No war. No crisis. Just a state that had run out of money — because two successive governments, BJP and Congress alike, competed to offer more free electricity, more cash transfers, more pension benefits. The voters rewarded them both. The bill came due. The people who run the state went unpaid.

This is not a Himachal Pradesh story. This is an India story.

## We Built This

Every year, India spends ₹7–10 lakh crore on subsidies and freebies. Nearly 20% of our entire national budget — gone before a single road is built, a single school is upgraded, or a single hospital bed is added. Meanwhile, 55 million court cases sit pending. Half our Class 5 children cannot read a Class 2 textbook. Only 28% of our sewage is treated before it enters our rivers.

These are not accidents. They are choices — made every budget cycle, every election season, by governments optimising for the next five months, not the next fifty years.

Look at Karnataka. Three freebie schemes launched in 2023. By 2024, property taxes went up and funds for roads and public health came down. Across six states that revised their budgets to accommodate new freebie schemes, all six increased revenue expenditure, five raised their fiscal deficit targets — but only two marginally increased capital expenditure. Development was literally the line item that got cut.

Here is what most people get wrong: the problem is not corrupt politicians.

A politician who hands out free electricity instead of building factories is not lazy or evil. He is rational. He is solving the problem in front of him — how do I win the next election? And the answer, every single time, has been the same. Give people something they can feel today. A gas cylinder. A cash transfer. A free bus ride.

These work because we let them work. Because we reward them.

## The Uncomfortable Truth

Politics is a market. Politicians are suppliers. We are the demand.

For decades, we have been demanding the wrong things — or worse, not demanding anything at all. We have voted for the hand that feeds us, not the hand that builds our future. So the system learned. It optimised. It got very good at giving us exactly what we asked for.

The freebie culture is not something that was done *to* us. It is something we built — one vote at a time, one election at a time, one unanswered question at a time.

The politician did not decide that a gas cylinder is worth more than a school. We did — every time we did not ask about the school.

## It Can Go the Other Way

History shows the reverse is possible.

The RTI Act of 2005 was not born in Parliament. It was born in a village in Rajasthan, where farmers and daily wage workers organised public hearings and demanded to know where the government's money was going. They sat in protest for 40 days. A decade later, India had one of the strongest transparency laws in the world.

When citizens organise around a clear demand, the political system eventually has to respond.

Eight states spent ₹2.11 lakh crore on direct cash transfers in a single year — more than India's entire annual education budget. That same money, redirected through organised civic pressure, could fund thousands of schools, clinics, and local infrastructure projects that actually compound over time.

The question is not whether change is possible. The question is who asks for it, and how.

## The Fix Being Proposed: Ward by Ward

Change will not come from the top. It never has.

It comes from the bottom — from enough people deciding, ward by ward, neighbourhood by neighbourhood, that they want something different. Not a different party. A different kind of politics altogether.

The idea is simple: shift the votebank. Right now, politicians win votes by targeting individuals with visible, immediate benefits. What if communities became the unit instead? What if a ward — as a collective — decided what it wanted, held its councillor to that standard, and voted accordingly?

A ward that holds its councillor accountable becomes a constituency that demands better. A constituency that demands better produces a different kind of politician. This is not idealism. It is arithmetic.

Start with what you can see. Your ward councillor. Your local school. The drain outside your building that floods every monsoon. These are not small things — they are the building blocks of everything larger.

## Help Build the Framework

This idea — the ward as the unit of change — needs to become more than an idea. It needs a framework. A way for communities to organise, identify what they want, hold their representatives to account, and do it again, and again, until it becomes the norm.

That framework does not exist yet. And we think it should be built by the people who will actually use it.
